
\subsection{Validating the relevance of hybrid models}

The steel study develops a hybrid architecture combining ARIMAX and Neural Networks in order to integrate both time-series dynamics and exogenous factors into a single forecasting engine. One of the most important findings of the M4 Competition was the victory of Slawek Smyl's ES-RNN model, which is described as a truly hybrid algorithm combining Exponential Smoothing (ES) with Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN). This result suggests that the hybrid approach used in the steel study is not merely an industry-specific solution, but may represent a broader standard for demand forecasting.

\subsection{Debunking the limits of pure Machine Learning}

Both articles reach a similar conclusion: pure Machine Learning (ML) models often perform poorly in time-series forecasting. The steel article points out that non-combined ML approaches are not able to deal with all sources of uncertainty. Likewise, the M4 article reports the poor performance of pure ML methods, which were less accurate than relatively simple statistical benchmarks. This finding reinforces the idea that combining statistical models with machine learning methods is often more effective than relying on ML alone.

\subsection{Addressing uncertainty and risk}

Finally, both articles are closely related in their shared attention to uncertainty management. The steel study emphasizes the importance of accounting for model, parameter, and data uncertainty through hybrid modeling. The M4 Competition extends this perspective by being the first to include \(95\%\) prediction intervals (PIs) in its evaluation, showing that hybrid models such as Smyl's can capture uncertainty with remarkable precision. This dimension is particularly important for a steel manufacturer's make-to-stock strategy, where understanding the possible range of demand is just as important as estimating the most likely outcome.
